## The Core Challenge

You have a holding company (QLS) with a flagship product (PRISM) that needs to launch _now_, but you don't want to paint yourself into a corner where QLS = PRISM. The site needs to:

1. **Establish QLS as the credible parent brand** with a clear philosophy
2. **Lead with PRISM** as the flagship, market-ready offering
3. **Signal depth** - hint at the broader ecosystem without over-promising
4. **Speak to the right audience** - compliance officers, CFOs, regulatory professionals

---

## The Unifying Narrative

Here's the thread that ties everything together:

> **"Systems track what happens. We understand _why_."**

Or, slightly more institutional:

> **"The architecture of decisions."**

This works because:

- PRISM: _Why_ do firms make these prudential choices?
- CRAMPT: _Why_ do compliance processes succeed or fail?
- BIE: _Why_ did the user hesitate, override, choose?
- FeelX: _Why_ does this music move you?
- ARGUS: _Why_ is this volatility environment favourable?

It's abstract enough to cover everything, concrete enough to mean something.

## Design Direction

Given your audience (compliance professionals, CFOs, regulatory types), I'd suggest:

**Aesthetic:**

- Clean, lots of whitespace
- Dark navy / deep blue as primary (trust, authority)
- Accent colour from the QLS logo palette (that amber/gold works well)
- Typography: Something with gravitas but not stuffy. Inter, IBM Plex Sans, or similar
- Minimal animation. These aren't people who want parallax scrolling.

**Tone:**

- Confident but not arrogant
- Technical but not jargon-heavy
- Institutional but not boring
- British understatement rather than American hyperbole

**What to Avoid:**

- "Revolutionising" / "Disrupting" / startup-speak
- Generic stock photos of people in suits
- Cluttered feature matrices
- Aggressive CTAs ("BUY NOW", "LIMITED TIME")

## Closing Thought on Positioning

The trick with QLS is that you're _not_ a typical fintech startup, and the site should reflect that. You're closer to a specialist consultancy that happens to build technology, rather than a technology company that happens to know about finance.

That positioning is actually _stronger_ for your target market. Compliance officers are deeply suspicious of tech companies who don't understand regulatory nuance. They'll trust "21 years in financial services, built by someone who's been there" far more than "AI-powered RegTech solution."

Lead with credibility. Let the technology speak through the product, not the marketing.
